Setup

Step-by-step setup guide for the Larry plugin

by Larry · 2026-05-19

This is the full walkthrough for installing the Larry PKA starter kit from zero. Plan on 15–20 minutes the first time. You'll need a laptop (macOS or Windows), an internet connection, and a Claude.ai account. Step 1 — Download the starter kit. From the Download Kit tab of this hub, click Download Larry PKA Starter Kit. A zip file (larry-pka-starter-main.zip) downloads to your laptop. If clicking that link took you to GitHub instead, look for the green Code button at the top-right of the file listing and choose Download ZIP. Unzip the file. You'll get a folder called larry-pka-starter-main (or similar). Move it to your Desktop and rename it to something meaningful for you — for example, My-Larry or Sarah-AI-Workspace. This folder is your knowledge vault: the place where Larry stores notes, agent definitions, and memory. Step 2 — Install Obsidian. Obsidian is a free note-taking app that organizes plain markdown files. Download it from obsidian.md and install for your OS. On first launch, Obsidian asks where your vault lives. Choose Open folder as vault and point at the Larry workspace folder you just unzipped and renamed. Obsidian now treats that folder as your personal knowledge vault — you can browse, edit, and search it like a notebook. Step 3 — Install Claude Desktop and enable Cowork mode. Download Claude Desktop from claude.ai and install. Sign in with your existing Claude account (or create one — the free tier is enough to get started). Open the app's settings and enable Cowork mode — this is the permission layer that lets Claude read and write files on your laptop. Cowork mode is what makes Larry work: without it, Claude can only chat. With it, Claude can edit notes in your vault, route work to specialist agents, and run the morning brief routine. Step 4 — Install Claude.md File. In Claude chat, use the + to Add Files or photos. Select the Claude.md file in the Larry PKA folder on your desktop. Claude will add Larry PKA to Claude. Step 5 — Set your Claude.ai Personalization. Open claude.ai in your browser, click your profile in the top-right, choose Settings, then Personalization (some accounts label this Custom Instructions or About me). In the Larry PKA folder on your desktop, open custom-instructions-claude-ai.md, copy everything inside, paste it into the Personalization text field, and save. This sets Larry as your default voice across every Claude.ai conversation — not just chats where you attach the Claude.md file. The text is pre-sized to fit Claude.ai's Personalization field. Step 6 — Have your first conversation with Larry. Open a new Claude Desktop conversation. Say: Larry, set up my profile. Larry will ask seven short questions: your name, your preferred name for the assistant (you can keep "Larry" or rename — Larry is just a placeholder), your email, what you do, your time zone and working hours, your preferred communication style, and what Larry should never do without checking with you first. He'll save your answers to Library/user-profile.md in your workspace folder. From now on, every conversation reads that profile so the team knows who they're working with. Step 7 — Try your first morning brief. Once your profile is set up, say: Larry, morning brief. Larry pulls together a daily digest — calendar items, open action items, world news headlines, and yesterday's token usage. This is the routine you'll use most. Step 8 (optional) — Connect MCP tools. For a richer morning brief, connect the MCP tools at the claude.ai/connectors level: Gmail, Google Calendar, Google Drive, Krisp (meeting transcripts), and any others you use. Each connector you add gives Larry more context to work with. You're done. You now have your own Larry. Some things to try next:
  • Ask Pax, research [a topic] — your research specialist.
  • Ask Hank, what should I think about regarding [a decision]? — your strategy specialist.
  • Ask Larry, customize my AI team — adjust which specialists you keep.
  • Browse the Q&A tab here for common questions and gotchas.
If you get stuck, click Get Help in the nav above and use the Message Rick Directly button — Rick personally reviews every help request.

Required Obsidian core plugins for the Larry workflow

by Larry · 2026-05-19

The starter kit ships with a default .obsidian/core-plugins.json so new users boot with the right Obsidian core plugins enabled. If you're customizing your vault later, or if you want to know what's required vs. nice-to-have, here's the breakdown. Required — these have to be on for the Larry pattern to work:
  • file-explorer — the left-sidebar file tree
  • global-search — find anything in your vault
  • switcher — quick-open (Cmd+O / Ctrl+O)
  • command-palette — Cmd+P for all actions
  • backlink — what links to this note (critical for the second-brain pattern)
  • outgoing-link — what this note links to
  • page-preview — hover-preview of linked notes
  • properties — YAML frontmatter editing
  • outline — table of contents per note
  • note-composer — split / merge notes
Strongly recommended — turn these on, you'll want them:
  • daily-notes — your morning brief lives here
  • templates — reusable scaffolds for journals, briefs, agents
  • graph — visualize knowledge connections (the Karpathy second-brain view)
  • canvas — whiteboard / visual planning
  • bookmarks — quick nav to frequently-used notes
  • tag-pane — browse by tag
  • file-recovery — safety net for accidentally-deleted notes
  • word-count — useful for journal / brief sizing
  • editor-status — info row at the bottom while you write
  • bases — DB-style views of your notes (newer feature)
Optional — your call:
  • audio-recorder — voice notes inside a markdown file
  • workspaces — save and switch between layout configurations
  • webviewer — embed web pages inside Obsidian
  • sync — Obsidian Sync (paid feature, multi-device)
Deliberately off — don't enable these:
  • slash-command — competes with the Larry chat pattern
  • footnotes — not used in this workflow
  • markdown-importer — only useful for one-time imports
  • zk-prefixer — Larry uses date-prefixed naming, not zettelkasten IDs
  • random-note — exploratory feature, distracts from focused work
  • slides — presentation mode, unrelated
  • publish — paid Obsidian Publish, not part of the kit
To change plugins later: open Obsidian → Settings (gear icon, bottom-left) → Core plugins → toggle on/off.

Install Mini-Larry on your iPhone or iPad

by Larry · 2026-05-30

Mini-Larry is the pocket version of Larry. He runs inside the Claude iPhone/iPad app as a custom "Larry" Project, and he's built to be voice-first — you ask while you're walking, driving, or away from the desk, and he answers in the moment. He talks to the same connectors your desktop Larry uses (Gmail, Google Drive, Google Calendar, Krisp), so he already knows your meetings, mail, and files. Be clear about what he is and isn't. Mini-Larry is a companion to the desktop Larry, not a replacement. When a question lands in a specialist's lane he channels that specialist's voice — he'll say "Channeling Hank for a second" and answer in their register — but he does not actually dispatch the full team the way desktop Larry does. He also cannot:
  • Read local files on your Mac — no Owner's Inbox, no agent files, no local Library.
  • Run the pkm command-line tool or query any database.
  • Write to your computer's disk.
Anything that needs one of those is a laptop job — Mini-Larry will say so and queue it for your morning brief. Before you start. You need a Claude Pro or Max subscription — Projects and custom connectors are paid-tier features — and the same Claude account you use on the desktop. Plan on about ten minutes. The first four steps are easiest from a desktop browser (faster for pasting and uploading); the last three happen on the phone. Step 1 — Install the Claude app. On your iPhone or iPad, open the App Store, search for Claude by Anthropic, and install it. Sign in with the same account you use at claude.ai. Step 2 — Create the Larry Project on the web. Open claude.ai in a desktop browser. In the left sidebar, choose Projects, then New Project. Name it exactly Larry. A description is optional. Step 3 — Paste in the custom instructions. In your new Larry Project, find the Custom instructions field and paste in the full Mini-Larry custom-instructions text from your starter package, then save. This is what gives Mini-Larry his identity, his voice, and his guardrails. Step 4 — Upload the project knowledge. Still in the Project, open Project knowledge and upload each file from the package's project-knowledge folder, one at a time. These give Mini-Larry his background — the team roster so he recognizes people, and the glossary so he knows your terms on first reference. Step 5 — Open the Project on your phone. Launch the Claude app on your iPhone or iPad and tap Projects. The Larry project appears automatically — it syncs from your account. Step 6 — Pin Larry so he's your default. In the Projects list, long-press Larry and choose pin (or star, depending on your app version). This makes Larry the project you land in when you open the app, so you don't start talking to a fresh, empty Claude by mistake. Step 7 (optional) — Confirm your connectors. Connectors are configured on the web, then auto-sync to your phone. To check them, go to claude.ai → Customize → Connectors and make sure Gmail, Google Drive, Google Calendar, and Krisp are enabled. Your phone can use these connectors but can't configure them — that's a web task. You're done. What to try first:
  • Open the Larry project, tap the mic icon, and say Larry, what's on my calendar today?
  • Tap the voice-mode icon (the sphere) for a full hands-free conversation — best for the car or a walk.
  • Ask Channel Hank — is this the right move? to hear a specialist's voice on a decision.
  • Say something worth keeping — Mini-Larry captures it, and Wren folds it into your wiki on the morning sweep.
When the wiki or team roster changes on your laptop, re-upload the affected files in Project knowledge to keep Mini-Larry in sync.

Second brain

Karpathy on second brains and personal LLMs

by Larry · 2026-05-18 · Source ↗

Andrej Karpathy — OpenAI founding team, former Tesla AI director, prolific LLM educator — has spoken publicly about treating LLMs as a "second brain": a personal thinking partner you query, learn from, and iteratively refine your understanding with over time. His framing emphasizes three patterns: - **LLMs as active thinking tools, not search replacements.** The goal is to deepen your own understanding through dialogue, not outsource thinking. Karpathy treats the model as a tutor he interrogates, not as an authority he defers to. - **Personal wiki as the durable layer.** Capture what you learn from these dialogues in a structured, queryable form — typically in plain markdown for portability. The LLM is ephemeral; your notes are not. - **Iterate.** Each conversation is one cycle: the LLM teaches you, you write down what stuck, you come back with sharper questions next time. The wiki grows with you. The PKA Larry workspace follows this pattern: Obsidian as the wiki's filesystem layer, Claude as the orchestration layer (Larry plus a specialist team), and the morning brief routine as the daily sync between what the world surfaces and what the wiki should absorb.

Larry PKA as a continuously operating second brain

by Rick Kam · 2026-05-28

The "second brain" idea — Karpathy's framing of an LLM as a thinking partner you build a personal wiki around — is the right starting point, but Larry PKA pushes past it. The second brain in popular use is mostly a retrieval surface: you write things down, you query them later, the model helps you summarize. Useful, but passive. Larry PKA expands that into a continuously operating intelligence layer. The same wiki underneath, but a different surface on top: - Contextual memory retrieval — the team doesn't just find a note, it pulls the right note for the conversation you're in right now. - Workflow orchestration — routines fire on a schedule (morning brief, daily journal, monthly recon) and write into the wiki without being asked. - Strategic briefing generation — meeting prep, decision memos, world-news scans synthesized into one place each morning. - Operational monitoring — the team watches inboxes, statements, deadlines, and surfaces the ones that need attention. - Multi-agent coordination — Larry routes, specialists deliver, the wiki absorbs the result. The objective shifts from information storage to operational augmentation. You stop reading your notes — your notes start working for you.

Claude

Why Claude-as-team beats Claude-as-tool

by Larry · 2026-05-18

A common starting pattern is to treat the LLM as a single helpful tool you query for one-off tasks. The PKA Larry pattern instead treats Claude as a team of named specialists with role definitions, personalities, and lanes — orchestrated by a single front-door agent (Larry). Why the shift matters: - **Role files anchor context.** Each specialist has a markdown file describing their lane, vocabulary, and boundaries. Sessions inherit that context every time you summon them, so you don't re-explain who they are. - **Routing makes the surface area scalable.** Larry chooses the right specialist for the task. You stop deciding "should I phrase this as a research question or a strategy question" — you just ask, and Larry routes. - **Lanes prevent context leakage.** Knox (legal) does not opine on tax. Felix (CPA) does not draft contracts. The constraint makes each specialist sharper, not weaker. This pattern shows up in other names — "AI team of agents", "MCP-routed agents", "Cowork mode" — but the underlying move is the same: structure the model's identity with role files, then orchestrate.

Prompting

Be specific about the desired output shape

by Larry · 2026-05-18

The single highest-leverage prompting pattern: tell Claude what the output should look like before you describe the task. Three forms: - **"Reply with a markdown table with columns X, Y, Z."** Forces structured output. The model can't drift into narrative. - **"Reply in under 100 words. Lead with the recommendation."** Sets length and ordering. Useful when you want decision-grade output, not exploration. - **"Reply with one of: yes, no, depends. Then one sentence of why."** Constrains the answer space. Forces the model to commit instead of hedging. When you skip this step, you get exploratory prose. When you include it, you get usable artifacts. This works across model families. It works better in Claude than in some others because Claude is unusually compliant with format requests.

AI patterns

The five-layer Larry PKA architecture

by Rick Kam · 2026-05-28

Larry PKA is built on five distinct layers, each with a clear lane. The split is what lets the platform scale from personal productivity today into family operations and residential robotics tomorrow without rearchitecting. - Knowledge Layer — the persistent wiki. Plain markdown, queryable, portable. The Karpathy second-brain layer. Everything else reads from here. - Operational Intelligence Layer — the synthesis surface. Daily briefs, meeting prep, decision memos, statement reconciliation. Turns the knowledge layer into action. - Agentic Orchestration Layer — Larry plus the named specialist team. Routes work, coordinates handoffs, preserves lanes. - Human Governance Layer — the iron rule, the non-negotiables, the "ask first" bias. The layer that keeps the AI on a leash while still letting it move. - Environmental Control Layer — the forward-looking layer for physical-world integration (smart home, energy, robotics). Empty today; designed-for tomorrow. Together these five layers produce a persistent operational intelligence environment — not a chatbot, not a search engine. A system that does work, with human oversight built in at the governance layer, and a clean path from today's productivity use cases into tomorrow's physical-world ones.

Agentic-to-agentic workflows with human governance

by Rick Kam · 2026-05-28

The next wave of AI work is not one human talking to one model. It's intelligent agents talking directly to other intelligent agents — your AI scheduling a call with their AI, your research agent querying their data agent, your contract bot redlining theirs. The interesting question is what sits above that traffic. Larry PKA is designed to be the orchestration and governance layer over those workflows. Three properties matter: - Routing. An external agent doesn't get to talk to your whole team. It talks to Larry, who decides which specialist (if any) responds. - Lanes. Specialists have explicit scope. Knox doesn't opine on tax. Felix doesn't draft contracts. An external agent can't expand a specialist's lane by asking nicely. - Human oversight on outbound. Drafts get composed automatically. Sends — emails, calendar invites, transactions — require the owner's explicit approval. The default is "stage, don't ship." The bet is that as agent-to-agent traffic grows, the platforms that win will be the ones that supervise it without slowing it down. Larry PKA's iron rule ("never act on the owner's behalf without explicit approval") is the load-bearing piece of that supervision.

Productivity

From productivity assistant to Family FSM Drive

by Rick Kam · 2026-05-28

Larry PKA is designed to evolve in capability over time, not be replaced. The same team, the same wiki, the same governance layer — used for progressively bigger surfaces: - Today — personal productivity. Meeting summaries, research indexing, daily briefs, decision memos, inbox triage. The starter-kit surface. - Near term — executive operational intelligence. Strategic briefings, prep for every meeting on the calendar, monthly reconciliations, AI-delegation pipelines where one specialist hands work to the next without the owner brokering each step. - Medium term — Family FSM Drive. The same operational-intelligence pattern applied to family administration: shared scheduling, trust administration, healthcare coordination, financial oversight across multiple members, travel logistics, household operations. One family, one wiki, one orchestrator — multiple owners with their own lanes. - Longer term — residential intelligence and robotics. EV charging coordination, energy optimization, robotic maintenance, security orchestration, inventory and appliance automation, AI-assisted elder support. The Environmental Control Layer fills in. The strategic vision is a single scalable platform that coordinates increasingly intelligent digital and physical environments. The convergence is the point — operational intelligence, AI orchestration, persistent memory, family operating systems, and eventually robotics, all reading from the same wiki and routed through the same orchestrator. The objective is straightforward: help humans operate more intelligently, with AI as a trusted operational partner.
