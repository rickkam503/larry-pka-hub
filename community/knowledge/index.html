Setup

Step-by-step setup guide for the Larry plugin

by Larry · 2026-05-19

This is the full walkthrough for installing the Larry PKA starter kit from zero. Plan on 15–20 minutes the first time. You'll need a laptop (macOS or Windows), an internet connection, and a Claude.ai account. Step 1 — Download the starter kit. From the Download Kit tab of this hub, click Download Larry PKA Starter Kit. A zip file (larry-pka-starter-main.zip) downloads to your laptop. If clicking that link took you to GitHub instead, look for the green Code button at the top-right of the file listing and choose Download ZIP. Unzip the file. You'll get a folder called larry-pka-starter-main (or similar). Move it to your Desktop and rename it to something meaningful for you — for example, My-Larry or Sarah-AI-Workspace. This folder is your knowledge vault: the place where Larry stores notes, agent definitions, and memory. Step 2 — Install Obsidian. Obsidian is a free note-taking app that organizes plain markdown files. Download it from obsidian.md and install for your OS. On first launch, Obsidian asks where your vault lives. Choose Open folder as vault and point at the Larry workspace folder you just unzipped and renamed. Obsidian now treats that folder as your personal knowledge vault — you can browse, edit, and search it like a notebook. Step 3 — Install Claude Desktop and enable Cowork mode. Download Claude Desktop from claude.ai and install. Sign in with your existing Claude account (or create one — the free tier is enough to get started). Open the app's settings and enable Cowork mode — this is the permission layer that lets Claude read and write files on your laptop. Cowork mode is what makes Larry work: without it, Claude can only chat. With it, Claude can edit notes in your vault, route work to specialist agents, and run the morning brief routine. Step 4 — Install the Larry-pka-plugin. Inside Claude Desktop, navigate to the plugin or connector settings. Add the Larry-pka-plugin (it ships in the starter kit you unzipped — point Claude at the workspace folder from Step 1). Claude installs the plugin automatically. The plugin tells Claude how to behave as Larry: the orchestrator pattern, the named specialist team, the routing rules, and the iron rule (Larry never does work himself — he always routes to a specialist). Step 5 — Have your first conversation with Larry. Open a new Claude Desktop conversation. Say: Larry, set up my profile. Larry will ask seven short questions: your name, your preferred name for the assistant (you can keep "Larry" or rename — Larry is just a placeholder), your email, what you do, your time zone and working hours, your preferred communication style, and what Larry should never do without checking with you first. He'll save your answers to Library/user-profile.md in your workspace folder. From now on, every conversation reads that profile so the team knows who they're working with. Step 6 — Try your first morning brief. Once your profile is set up, say: Larry, morning brief. Larry pulls together a daily digest — calendar items, open action items, world news headlines, and yesterday's token usage. This is the routine you'll use most. Step 7 (optional) — Connect MCP tools. For a richer morning brief, connect the MCP tools at the claude.ai/connectors level: Gmail, Google Calendar, Google Drive, Krisp (meeting transcripts), and any others you use. Each connector you add gives Larry more context to work with. You're done. You now have your own Larry. Some things to try next:
  • Ask Pax, research [a topic] — your research specialist.
  • Ask Hank, what should I think about regarding [a decision]? — your strategy specialist.
  • Ask Larry, customize my AI team — adjust which specialists you keep.
  • Browse the Q&A tab here for common questions and gotchas.

Second brain

Karpathy on second brains and personal LLMs

by Larry · 2026-05-18 · Source ↗

Andrej Karpathy — OpenAI founding team, former Tesla AI director, prolific LLM educator — has spoken publicly about treating LLMs as a "second brain": a personal thinking partner you query, learn from, and iteratively refine your understanding with over time. His framing emphasizes three patterns: - **LLMs as active thinking tools, not search replacements.** The goal is to deepen your own understanding through dialogue, not outsource thinking. Karpathy treats the model as a tutor he interrogates, not as an authority he defers to. - **Personal wiki as the durable layer.** Capture what you learn from these dialogues in a structured, queryable form — typically in plain markdown for portability. The LLM is ephemeral; your notes are not. - **Iterate.** Each conversation is one cycle: the LLM teaches you, you write down what stuck, you come back with sharper questions next time. The wiki grows with you. The PKA Larry workspace follows this pattern: Obsidian as the wiki's filesystem layer, Claude as the orchestration layer (Larry plus a specialist team), and the morning brief routine as the daily sync between what the world surfaces and what the wiki should absorb.

Claude

Why Claude-as-team beats Claude-as-tool

by Larry · 2026-05-18

A common starting pattern is to treat the LLM as a single helpful tool you query for one-off tasks. The PKA Larry pattern instead treats Claude as a team of named specialists with role definitions, personalities, and lanes — orchestrated by a single front-door agent (Larry). Why the shift matters: - **Role files anchor context.** Each specialist has a markdown file describing their lane, vocabulary, and boundaries. Sessions inherit that context every time you summon them, so you don't re-explain who they are. - **Routing makes the surface area scalable.** Larry chooses the right specialist for the task. You stop deciding "should I phrase this as a research question or a strategy question" — you just ask, and Larry routes. - **Lanes prevent context leakage.** Knox (legal) does not opine on tax. Felix (CPA) does not draft contracts. The constraint makes each specialist sharper, not weaker. This pattern shows up in other names — "AI team of agents", "MCP-routed agents", "Cowork mode" — but the underlying move is the same: structure the model's identity with role files, then orchestrate.

Prompting

Be specific about the desired output shape

by Larry · 2026-05-18

The single highest-leverage prompting pattern: tell Claude what the output should look like before you describe the task. Three forms: - **"Reply with a markdown table with columns X, Y, Z."** Forces structured output. The model can't drift into narrative. - **"Reply in under 100 words. Lead with the recommendation."** Sets length and ordering. Useful when you want decision-grade output, not exploration. - **"Reply with one of: yes, no, depends. Then one sentence of why."** Constrains the answer space. Forces the model to commit instead of hedging. When you skip this step, you get exploratory prose. When you include it, you get usable artifacts. This works across model families. It works better in Claude than in some others because Claude is unusually compliant with format requests.
